About Me

Hi!! I am Albert Einstien and I was born on March 14, 1879 in the small city of Ulm, Germany. My father, Hermann, worked with electrical equipment, while my mother, Pauline, was a talented pianist who adored music. My younger sister and lifelong companion, Maja, was two years my junior.

I am best known for developing the theory of relativity and making significant contributions to quantum mechanics, thus playing a central role in revolutionizing the scientific understanding of nature in the early twentieth century. The mass-energy equivalence formula E=mc^2, which stems from relativity theory, is often referred to as "The world's most famous equation." I was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921 for my contributions to theoretical physics, particularly for discovering the law of the photoelectric effect, a crucial advancement in quantum theory.

Papers

Special Theory of Relativity

Year:1905

  • On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies.

  • Introduced the concepts of time dilation and length contraction, challenging classical notions of space and time.

Photoelectric Effect

Year:1905

  • On a Heuristic Viewpoint Concerning the Production and Transformation of Light.

  • Proposed that light consists of discrete packets of energy called photons, which laid the groundwork for quantum theory.

Brownian Motion

Year:1905

  • On the Movement of Small Particles Suspended in a Stationary Liquid Demanded by the Molecular-Kinetic Theory of Heat.

  • Explained the erratic motion of particles in a fluid as evidence of the existence of atoms and molecules, supporting the atomic theory.

Mass-Energy Equivalence

Year:1905

  • Does the Inertia of a Body Depend Upon Its Energy Content?

  • Introduced the famous equation E=mc^2, which demonstrates the equivalence of mass and energy and has profound implications for nuclear physics.
